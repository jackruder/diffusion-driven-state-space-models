* Model
For this doc, we drop superscript notation, where it is understood that \(p = p^{(t)}\) or \(p = p^{(t, k)}\) depending on context. We present \(j = 1\) for notational simplicity as well.
Our generative model is given by
\[p(z_{1:T}) = p(z_{1}) Π_{t=2}^T p(z_t | z_{t-1} ) p(x_t | z_t), \]
where provably the optimal variational posterior takes the form
\[ q(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T}) = q(z_1 | x_{1:T}) \prod_{t=2}^T q(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) \].

The associated ELBO contains the per-\(t\) term
\[\sum_{t=2}^T\mathrm{KL}\left[q_{φ}(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) \| p_{ψ}(z_t | z_{t-1})\right].\]
Our largest modeling choice of interest is to model \(p_ψ\) with a diffusion model. 
However, compared to other DVAE, this immediately creates two issues.

* Issues
** Lack of Closed-Form KL divergence.
When \(q_{φ}\) and \(p_{φ}\) are both Gaussian, the KL divergence is available in closed form.
We restrict to the setting where \(q_{φ}\) is Gaussian, and \(p_{φ}\) is not available, in which case we split
\[\mathrm{KL}\left[q_{φ}(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) \| p_{ψ}(z_t | z_{t-1})\right] = -H(q_{φ}) + \mathbb{E}_{q_{φ}}\left[ - \log p_{ψ}(z_t | z_{t-1}) \right].\]
The first term is the entropy, analytically available in closed form. 
The second term, the cross-entropy, *must* be estimated using Monte-Carlo samples drawn from \(q_{φ}\). 
*** DDPM objective (our current implementation)
Under a DDPM noise-reparameterization, the cross-entropy term has an upper-bound of 
\[\mathbb{E}_{q_φ}\!\left[-\log p_ψ(z_t | z_{t-1})\right] \leq \mathbb{E}_{q_φ}\!\left[L_K + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \tilde{L}_k + L_0\right],\]
where, using the noise-prediction network \(\epsilon_ψ\) and the forward-process sample \(z_t^k = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_k}\, z_t + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_k}\,\epsilon\), \(\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\):
- \(L_K = \mathrm{KL}[q_{\mathrm{fwd}}(z_t^K | z_t) \| \pi(z_t^K)]\), a constant w.r.t. \(\psi\),
- \(\tilde{L}_k = \frac{\beta_k^2}{2 \sigma_k^2 \alpha_k (1-\bar{\alpha}_k)} \left\| \epsilon - \epsilon_ψ(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_k}\, z_t + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_k}\,\epsilon,\; k,\; z_{t-1}) \right\|^2\) for \(k = 1, \ldots, K\),
- \(L_0 = -\log p_ψ(z_t | z_t^1, z_{t-1})\).
Minimizing this bound produces a likelihood estimate on \(p_{ψ}(z_{t } | z_{t-1})\) used in the overall ELBO. 

Given \(S\) trajectory samples \(z_{1:T}^{(s)} \sim q_φ(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})\), each providing a single \(z_t^{(s)}\) per time step, and for each \((s, t)\), \(S_k\) uniformly drawn noise levels \(k^{(s,i)} \sim \mathrm{Uniform}\{1, \ldots, K\}\) with independent noise \(\epsilon^{(s,i)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\), the MC estimator is
\[\mathbb{E}_{q_φ}\!\left[-\log p_ψ(z_t | z_{t-1})\right] \approx \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S}\!\left[L_K(z_t^{(s)}) + \frac{K}{S_k}\sum_{i=1}^{S_k} \tilde{L}_{k^{(s,i)}}(z_t^{(s)}, \epsilon^{(s,i)}) + L_0(z_t^{(s)})\right].\]

Note that the \(S\) samples are *trajectory-level*: we draw \(S\) full paths \(z_{1:T}^{(s)} \sim q_φ(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})\), each providing a single \(z_t^{(s)}\) per time step (conditioned on its own \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\)). This avoids the combinatorial \(S^T\) explosion that would arise from drawing \(S\) independent samples at every \(t\).

**** Gradients w.r.t. \(\psi\) (generative / denoiser parameters)
The entropy \(H(q_φ)\) does not depend on \(\psi\). 
The term \(L_K = \mathrm{KL}[q_{\mathrm{fwd}}(z_t^K | z_t) \| \pi(z_t^K)]\) involves only the fixed forward process and prior, so it also carries no \(\psi\)-gradient. 
The remaining terms give the gradient of the per-step transition-KL sum (\(t \geq 2\); the \(t=1\) term \(\mathrm{KL}[q_φ(z_1 \mid x_{1:T}) \| p_ψ(z_1)]\) is treated separately):
\[\nabla_ψ \sum_{t=2}^{T} \mathrm{KL}_t \approx \sum_{t=2}^{T} \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S}\!\left[\frac{K}{S_k}\sum_{i=1}^{S_k} \nabla_ψ \tilde{L}_{k^{(s,t,i)}}(z_t^{(s)}, \epsilon^{(s,t,i)}) + \nabla_ψ L_0(z_t^{(s)}, z_{t-1}^{(s)})\right],\]
where \(z_t^{(s)}\) and \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\) are fixed w.r.t \(\psi\). 
Note that \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\) enters as a conditioning input to both \(\epsilon_ψ\) and \(p_ψ\) in \(L_0\), but carries no \(\psi\)-gradient. 
We sum over every time step \(t\); only the diffusion noise levels \(k\) are subsampled (\(S_k\) per \((s, t)\)).

***** Sources of variance in \(\nabla_ψ\)
Since \(z_t^{(s)}\) and \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\) are held fixed w.r.t. \(\psi\), the gradient is a simple average over trajectory samples and noise-level samples. 
For a given time step \(t\):
1. *Trajectory sampling* (\(S\)): different \(z_t^{(s)}\) yield different examples for the denoiser. This variance in these examples reflects the wideness \(q_φ\), and compounds along latent chain (later \(t\) inherit noise from \(\eta_{1:t}^{(s)}\)).
2. *Noise-level sampling* (\(S_k\)): the gradient \(\nabla_ψ \tilde{L}_k\) varies across \(k\) because different noise levels probe different aspects of the denoiser. Without preconditioning, \(\tilde{L}_k\) is non-uniform across \(k\) (intermediate levels dominate), creating a large source of variance.
3. *Forward-diffusion noise* (\(\epsilon\)): for a fixed \((z_t, k)\), the specific \(\epsilon\) draw determines the noised input to \(\epsilon_ψ\). This is typically the smallest contribution.

Since \(\psi\) does not interact with the trajectory sampling process, these three sources are cleanly separated and no gradient amplification occurs across time steps.

**** Gradients w.r.t. \(\phi\) (encoder / variational parameters)
Using the reparameterization trick along the trajectory, each \(z_t^{(s)}\) depends on the full chain of noise variables \(\eta_1^{(s)}, \ldots, \eta_t^{(s)}\) via
\[z_t^{(s)} = \mu_φ(z_{t-1}^{(s)}, x_{t:T}) + \sigma_φ(z_{t-1}^{(s)}, x_{t:T}) \odot \eta_t^{(s)}, \quad \eta_t^{(s)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I), \quad t = 1, \ldots, T.\]
Every term in the per-step KL depends on \(\phi\):
1. The entropy \(H(q_φ(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}))\): analytic given \(z_{t-1}\), but \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\) itself depends on \(\phi\) through the previous trajectory.
2. \(L_K(z_t^{(s)})\): depends on \(\phi\) through \(z_t^{(s)}\), which determines the forward-diffusion endpoint. Though constant w.r.t. \(\psi\), it provides a \(\phi\)-gradient that pushes \(q_φ\) toward regions where the diffusion prior is well-matched. In practice this term is very, very small.
3. \(\tilde{L}_k(z_t^{(s)}, \epsilon^{(s,t,i)})\): depends on \(\phi\) through both \(z_t^{(s)}\) (the clean sample being noised) and \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\) (the conditioning input to \(\epsilon_ψ\)).
4. \(L_0(z_t^{(s)}, z_{t-1}^{(s)})\): depends on \(\phi\) through both \(z_t^{(s)}\) and \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\).

The full \(φ\)-gradient over the per-step transition KLs (\(t \geq 2\); the \(t=1\) term is treated separately, as above) is
\[\nabla_φ \sum_{t=2}^{T} \mathrm{KL}_t \approx \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S} \nabla_φ \sum_{t=2}^{T}\!\left[-H(q_φ(z_t^{(s)} | z_{t-1}^{(s)}, x_{t:T})) + L_K(z_t^{(s)}) + \frac{K}{S_k}\sum_{i=1}^{S_k} \tilde{L}_{k^{(s,t,i)}}(z_t^{(s)}, \epsilon^{(s,t,i)}) + L_0(z_t^{(s)}, z_{t-1}^{(s)})\right],\]
where \(\nabla_φ\) is computed by backpropagation through the full trajectory \(z_1^{(s)} \to \cdots \to z_T^{(s)}\). Every time step \(t\) is evaluated; only the diffusion noise levels \(k\) are subsampled (\(S_k\) per \((s,t)\), corrected by \(\frac{K}{S_k}\)). Each term at time \(t\) receives gradient contributions from all subsequent terms \(t' > t\) through the shared trajectory.

***** Sources of variance in \(\nabla_\phi\)
The \(\phi\)-gradient has the same three MC noise sources as \(\nabla_ψ\) (trajectory, noise-level, forward-diffusion), but with critical differences:
1. *Trajectory sampling* (\(S\)): as with \(\nabla_ψ\), different \(z_{1:T}^{(s)}\) yield different gradient estimates. However, the \(\phi\)-gradient at time \(t\) flows through the full trajectory via the Jacobians \(∂ z_t / ∂ z_r\) for \(r < t\). These Jacobian products may be a source of variance growing with \(t\); whether the growth is exponential depends on the spectrum of \(∂ z_r/∂ z_{r-1}\). /TODO: quantify — for a linear-Gaussian transition with Jacobian \(A\), the product is \(A^{t-r}\) and the variance contribution scales as \(\|A\|^{2(t-r)}\), giving exponential growth when \(\|A\| > 1\). For the MLP/DKF case the rate depends on activation choice and weight norms; cite or back-of-envelope./
2. *Noise-level sampling* (\(S_k\)): same as for \(\nabla_ψ\) — non-uniformity of \(\tilde{L}_k\) across \(k\) is the dominant inner-noise source at \(S_k = 1\).
3. *Forward-diffusion noise* (\(\epsilon\)): same as for \(\nabla_ψ\), not a major contribution.
4. *Trajectory sampling amplification*: unique to \(\nabla_\phi\). The loss at time \(t'\) contributes to \(\nabla_\phi\) at time \(t < t'\) through the product \(\prod_{r=t+1}^{t'} ∂ z_r / ∂ z_{r-1}\).
   This couples all three noise sources across time steps and can make variance grow with the sequence length \(T\). 


*** New objective.
There are two primary motivations for reformulating the objective.
**** Noise Scheduling (a source of variance)
First, we can control the noise schedule, which is a major source of variance. For example, in the VP SDE (which /does/ correspond to DDPM),
the variance of the ending noise process is identical to the data, where the VE SDE sends the variance to infinity. The LSGM paper describes that the Sub-VPSDE (presented in the Song SDE paper) reduces the variance in a helpful way for end-to-end training.
**** Principled Importance Sampling
Second, we can couple the objective with importance sampling techniques. These were found to be useful in the LSGM paper, where they train the diffusion alongside a VAE. Technically, we could employ IS to DDPM, but
it has already been well-explored by the LSGM paper, where it is easier to perform the analysis of the variance in a score-based setting. LSGM show the derivation of an effective importance sampling schedule derived from the VPSDE, and apply it to the Sub-VPSDE. See figures 2 and 6 from their paper. 
The authors also derive a Geometric-progression variance SDE, which is similar to the VESDE in the noise evolution but satisfies the variance-preserving property.
The geometric VPSDE /without/ IS is competitive with the VPSDE /with/ IS.
**** Potential Access to Explicit score matching? 
Finally, note that the form of score matching used in the LSGM paper above is denoising-score matching. However, the authors restrict themselves to this setting because they consider normalizing flows for
\(q(z | x)\).
Assuming that our choice of using a gaussian for \(q_{φ}(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T})\) (writing ASSUMING here to demonstrate an advantage of this choice, I think there are differences between using a Gaussian in the static case compared to here) is justified, we may be able to use explicit score matching.

In explicit score matching, you would compute \(J_{\mathrm{ESM}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_{q_{τ}(\overline{z})}\!\left[\|s_{θ}(\overline{z}) - ∇_{\overline{z}}\log q_{τ}(\overline{z})\|^{2}\right]\) where \(q_{τ}\) is the marginal noised distribution.
But knowing the score requires knowing the marginal, hence the original score matching paper (Hyvarinen) transfroms the objective to rely on a trace estimator. 
This is still expensive, so the modern approach typically employed by diffusion models is to employ denoising score matching, where only
\[J_{\mathrm{DSM}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_{q(z_t)}\mathbb{E}_{q_{τ}(\overline{z} | z_{t})}\!\left[\|s_{θ}(\overline{z}) - ∇_{\overline{z}}\log q_{τ}(\overline{z} | z_{t})\|^2\right]\]

The Vincent DSM paper shows that these objectives differ by a constant, IE \(J_{DSM} = J_{ESM} + C\).
Further, see recent analysis by Zhang et al. (2025, [[https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.01597][Why Heuristic Weighting Works: A Theoretical Analysis of Denoising SScore Matching]]), where this constant is shown (for a fixed noise level \(σ_t\)) to be
\(\frac{D}{σ_{t^2}} - \mathrm{tr}(\mathcal{I}(σ_t))\), where \(\mathcal{I}(σ_t) = \mathbb{E}_{x_t}\left[s(x_t)s(x_t)^T\right]\) is hte Fisher information of \(x_t\) and \(s\) is the score at \(x_t\).
They explain that as \(σ_t\) is close to zero in diffusion models, this constant may explode.
That is, ESM explicitly has lower variance than DSM.


Instead, observe that unlike most diffusion models, we actually have access to the target score function in closed form. The encoder gives the marginal score for free! Thus we can
use explicit score matching and we lose variance due to extremely high noise levels.
For simplicity of notation, we fix \(t\). Let \(z_{t-1},x_{t:T}\) be a conditioning set, and consider a continuous-time forward diffusion process, which takes an input \(z_t\) and noises it to \(z_{t}^{(τ)}\) for \(τ \in [0,1]\).
Let's consider the variance-preserving SDE \(dz_{t}^{(τ)} = -\frac{1}{2}β(τ)\,z_{t}^{(τ)} \; dτ + \sqrt{β(τ)}\;dw\). Then, the perturbation kernel \(q_{0τ}(z_{t}^{(τ)} | z_t)\) is normally distributed as
\(\mathcal{N}(α_{τ}z_t, σ_{τ}^{2}I)\) where \(α_{τ} = e^{-\tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^τ β(s)\,ds}\) and \(σ_{τ}^2 = 1 - α_τ^{2}\).
From our variational posterior, we have that \(q(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) = \mathcal{N}(μ_t, \boldsymbol{σ}_t^{2})\).
The marginal at diffusion time \(τ\) is then a Gaussian convolution. Sample
\(z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(μ_t, \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2)\) and \(ξ \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\) independently;
the forward kernel gives \(z_t^{(τ)} = α_τ z_t + σ_τ ξ\). Both right-hand-side terms are
independent Gaussians, so
\begin{align}
q_{φ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) &= \int q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t)q(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) dz_t \\
  &= \mathcal{N}\!\left(α_τ μ_t,\; α_τ^2 \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + σ_τ^2\right),
\end{align}
i.e. mean \(α_τ μ_t\) and per-dim variance \(α_τ^2 \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + σ_τ^2\) (the
scalar \(σ_τ^2\) broadcasts).

Then, we have
\begin{equation}
∇_{z_t^{(τ)}} \log q_{φ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) = -\frac{z_t^{(τ)} - α_{τ}μ_t}{α_{τ}^2\boldsymbol{σ}_t^2  + σ_{τ}^2}.
\end{equation}

Instead, we score match against the preceeding equation.

** ELBO modifications
For the score-matching theory to work and be precise, we need to simplify the ELBO.

The sequential ELBO for our generative model contains the terms

\begin{align*}&𝔼_{q_φ(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})}\left[\log q_{φ}(z_1 | x_{1:T}) - \log p_{ψ}(z_{1})\right] \\ &+𝔼_{q_{φ}(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})}\sum_{t=2}^T\mathrm{KL}\left[q_φ(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) \| p_ψ(z_t | z_{t-1})\right].\end{align*}

*I missed a simplification which is present in the Krishnan 2015 Deep Kalman Filters paper, however, the simplification I thought was insufficiently justified in that paper. I haven't checked other papers for this simplification but I'm sure it is known.*

The key simplification is due to the following simple result. *Probably exists somewhere, but I couldn't find the exact statement I liked.
*Lemma.* Let \(q(z_{1:T} | x)\) be a joint density on \(\mathcal{Z}_{1} × \mathcal{Z}_{2} × \cdots × \mathcal{Z}_{T} \) and let \(f : \mathcal{Z}_S \to ℝ\) be a measurable function from \(\mathcal{Z}_{S } :=  Π_{j ∈ S}\mathcal{Z}_j\) for some subset \(S ⊆ \left\{ 1, …, T \right\}\).
If \(f\) is integrable, then
\begin{equation*}
𝔼_{q(z_{1:T} | x)}\left[ |f(z_S)| \right] = 𝔼_{q(z_{S} | x)}\left[ |f(z_S)| \right],
\end{equation*}
where \(q(z_S | x) = ∫_{\mathcal{Z}_{S^c}} q(z_{1:T} | x)\, dz_{S^c}\) is the marginal.
*Proof.*
Since \(f\) is integrable, Fubini-Tonelli applies and thus
\begin{align*}
𝔼_{q(z_{1:T} | x)}\left[ |f(z_S)| \right]
&= ∫_{\mathcal{Z}_{S}}  \left[∫_{{\mathcal{Z}_{S^c}}}  f(z_S)q(z_{1:T} | x) dz_{S^c} \right] dz_S \\
&= ∫_{\mathcal{Z}_{S}} f(z_S) \left[ ∫_{{\mathcal{Z}_{S^c}}} q(z_{1:T} | x) dz_{S^c} \right] dz_S \\
&= ∫_{\mathcal{Z}_{S}} f(z_S)\, q(z_S | x)\, dz_S \\
&= 𝔼_{q(z_S | x)} \left[f(z_S)\right]. \\
\end{align*}

The implication of the above lemma is that the expectations in the ELBO simplify, since the KL divergence are deterministic given \(z_{t-1}\) and the observations. Therefore,



\begin{align*}
&𝔼_{q_φ(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})}\left[\log q_{φ}(z_1 | x_{1:T}) - \log p_{ψ}(z_{1})\right] \\ &+𝔼_{q_{φ}(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})}\sum_{t=2}^T\mathrm{KL}\left[q_φ(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) \| p_ψ(z_t | z_{t-1})\right] \\
&= 𝔼_{q_φ(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})}\left[\log q_{φ}(z_1 | x_{1:T}) - \log p_{ψ}(z_{1})\right] \\ &+ \sum_{t=2}^T𝔼_{q_{φ}(z_{1:T} | x_{1:T})}\mathrm{KL}\left[q_φ(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) \| p_ψ(z_t | z_{t-1})\right] \\
&= \mathrm{KL}\left[ q_{φ}(z_1 | x_{1:T}) \|  p_{ψ}(z_{1})\right] \\ &+ \sum_{t=2}^T𝔼_{q_{φ}(z_{t-1} | x_{1:T})}\mathrm{KL}\left[q_φ(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) \| p_ψ(z_t | z_{t-1})\right] \\
\end{align*}


The point of writing this way, compared to before, is that it is clear that the conditioning variables are fixed for the inner KL divergence, and we do not need to consider interactions between different \(z_t\) once a MC sample of \(z_{t-1}\) is obtained.

Note that this is equivilant to the derivation presented in Krishnan (2015).

** ESM Formulation
*** Notation:
Three distinct variance objects appear in what follows; we solidify notation here for
reference.
1. /Encoder posterior variance/ \(\boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 = \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2(z_{t-1}, x_{t:T})\):
   the per-dimension diagonal output by \(q_φ\). No diffusion-time
   dependence.
2. /Per-sample marginal variance at diffusion time \(τ\)/
   \(Σ_t^{(τ)} := \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2\)
   (per-dim vector; the scalar \(\tilde{σ}_τ^2\) broadcasts over the
   \(D\) dimensions; in native coordinates the additive term is
   \(σ_τ^2\) instead): the variance of \(z_t^{(τ)}\) given the
   conditioning \((z_{t-1}, x_{t:T})\). Absorbs the diffusion noise.
3. /Data-marginal variance of the clean latent/
   \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t) := \tfrac{1}{D}\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{Var}_{p_{\mathrm{data}}, q_φ}[\hat{z}_t^{(0)}])\):
   the variance of the (centered) clean latent under the joint over
   data and encoder noise, at \(τ = 0\). Similar to \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}\)
   used by EDM in the preconditioning constants. Estimated by
   \(\bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) per batch (see /Data-variance
   tracking/).

These are all distinct: (1) is per-sample and per-dim, (2) adds
diffusion noise to (1), and (3) is a scalar marginal over the joint
\((p_{\mathrm{data}}, q_φ)\).

*** Closed-form target score

We have \(q_φ(z_t | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) = \mathcal{N}(z_t; μ_t, \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2)\) with
\((μ_t, \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2) = (μ_t(z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}), \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2(z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}))\).
Assume the forward SDE has Gaussian perturbation kernel
\(q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t) = \mathcal{N}(α_τ z_t, σ_τ^2 I)\).
Then, the marginal is Gaussian:
\[q_φ^{(τ)}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) = \mathcal{N}\left(z_t^{(τ)};\; α_τ μ_t,\; α_τ^2 \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + σ_τ^2\right),\]
hence
\[s_q^{(τ)} = -\frac{z_t^{(τ)} - α_τ μ_t(z_{t-1}, x_{t:T})}{α_τ^2 \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2(z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) + σ_τ^2}.\]

We therefore may perform /*explicit* score matching/: \(s_q^{(τ)}\) is available analytically as a function
of the encoder outputs \((μ_t, \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2)\), with no denoising / noise-prediction
reparameterization needed. Contrast with LSGM, which uses DSM because their \(q\)-marginal
is not tractable.

*** ESM has lower variance than DSM.
The marginal score is the conditional expectation of the
kernel score under the posterior over the clean latent.
Writing \(c := (z_{t-1}, x_{t:T})\) and using the noise-kernel factorization
\(q_φ^{(τ)}(z_t^{(τ)} | c) = ∫ q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t)\, q_φ(z_t | c)\, dz_t\),
we compute the marginal score step by step. (This is a well-known derivation)

First, push the gradient through the integral and use
\(∇_{z_t^{(τ)}} q_{0τ} = q_{0τ}\, ∇_{z_t^{(τ)}} \log q_{0τ}\):
\begin{align*}
s_q^{(τ)}(z_t^{(τ)} | c)
& = ∇_{z_t^{(τ)}} \log q_φ^{(τ)}(z_t^{(τ)} | c) \\
&= \frac{∫ ∇_{z_t^{(τ)}} q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t)\, q_φ(z_t | c)\, dz_t}{q_φ^{(τ)}(z_t^{(τ)} | c)} \\
&= \frac{∫ q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t)\, q_φ(z_t | c)\, ∇_{z_t^{(τ)}} \log q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t)\, dz_t}{q_φ^{(τ)}(z_t^{(τ)} | c)}.
\end{align*}
Then, identifying

1. \(q(z^{(τ)}, τ)\), \(\frac{∂q}{∂τ}\), and \(\frac{∂}{∂τ}[q \log q]\) are continuous in both arguments,
2. There exist integrable dominating functions \(h_1(z^{(τ)}), h_2(z^{(τ)})\), uniform in \(τ\) on any compact interval, such that \(\left|\frac{∂}{∂τ}(q \log q)\right| ≤ h_1\) and \(\left|\frac{∂q}{∂τ}\right| ≤ h_2\) for all \(τ\).
3. \(fq \log q → 0\) as \(\|z^{(τ)}\| → ∞\).

4. \(q, ∇ q → 0\) sufficiently fast as \(\|z^{(τ)}\| → ∞\).
These conditions permit two applications of the Leibniz rule: one to bring \(∂_τ\) inside \(∫ q \log q \, dz^{(τ)}\), and one to bring it inside \(∫ q \, dz^{(τ)}\). Assuming these regularity conditions hold, we have
\begin{align*}
-\frac{∂H}{∂τ} &= ∫ \frac{∂}{∂τ}(q \log q) \, dz^{(τ)} \\

               &= ∫ \frac{∂q}{∂τ} \log q \, dz^{(τ)},

** SDE as a generative model
We model the transition density \(p_{ψ}(z_t | z_{t-1} )\) with one induced by reversing a forward noising SDE.

The key observation is that Anderson's time-reversal theorem is agnostic to conditioning, since the drift and diffusion coefficients of the forward SDE are not dependent on the conditioning set.
*** Forward SDE.
We fix \(τ\) and introduce the diffusion time \(τ \in [0,1]\).
We let \(z_t^{(τ)}\) denote the state of the forward process governed by
\begin{equation*}
dz_t^{(τ)} = f(z_t^{(τ)}, τ)dτ + g(τ)dw,
\end{equation*}
where \(w\) is standard Brownian motion and we write \(z_{t}^{(0)}:= z_t\), where for generality we assume \(z_t\) is given by a density \(z_t ~ ρ(z_t)\). The density under the diffusion process at time \(τ\) we denote by \(ρ^{τ}(z_t)\).
As usual, the drift \(f\) and diffusion \(g\) are chosen such that \(ρ_1(z_t^{(1)})\) is close to \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\).
Assuming linear drift \(f(z,τ)=h(τ)z\), the perturbation kernel is Gaussian,
\(q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t) = \mathcal{N}(α_τ z_t, σ_τ^2 I)\) where  \(α_τ = \exp\!∫_0^{τ} h(s)\,ds\) and \( σ_τ^2 = α_τ^2 ∫_0^{τ} \frac{g^2(s)}{α_s^2}\,ds.\)

*** Reverse SDE via Anderson's Theorem.
Anderson's theorem states that for any forward Ito SDE with coefficients \((f,g)\) and density \(ρ_{τ}\), the time-reversed process satisfies
\(dz_t^{(τ)} = \left[ f(z_t^{(τ)}, τ) - g^2(τ)∇_{z_t^{(τ)}}\log ρ_{τ}(z_t^{(τ)}) \right]dτ + g(τ)d\bar{w},\)
where \(d\bar{w}\) is a reverse time standard brownian motion from \(τ=1\) to \(τ=0\).
The theorem holds for /any/ density \(ρ_{τ}\) under the forward SDE. That is, conditioning on a fixed variable \(c\) yields the diffusion-time density \(ρ_{τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | c)\) and then 
\(dz_t^{(τ)} = \left[ f(z_t^{(τ)}, τ) - g^2(τ)∇_{z_t^{(τ)}}\log ρ_{τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | c) \right]dτ + g(τ)d\bar{w}.\)
Thus, the conditioning variable enters solely through the marginal score \(ρ_{τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | c)\).

**** Change of Coordinates
We adopt the preconditioning scheme of EDM to reparamaterize our explicit score-matching objective. 
The EDM framework presents the forward process using the VE kernel

Then, rescaling the kernel
\(q_{0τ}(z_t^{(τ)} | z_t) = \mathcal{N}(α_τ z_t,

\[\tilde{q}_{0τ}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} | z_t) = \mathcal{N}(z_t, \tilde{σ}_τ^2 I).\]

We work out how the score-matching identity transforms under the
change of variables \(\tilde{z} = z/α_τ\). The Jacobian determinant of
\(z \mapsto z/α_τ\) is \(α_τ^{-D}\), so the densities are related by
\[
\tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}) \;=\; α_τ^D \, q_φ^{(τ)}(α_τ \tilde{z}),
\]
which on log-densities becomes
\(\log \tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}) = \log q_φ^{(τ)}(α_τ \tilde{z}) + D \log α_τ\).
Taking \(∇_{\tilde{z}}\) (the additive \(D \log α_τ\) constant drops):
\[
\tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}) \;:=\; ∇_{\tilde{z}} \log \tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}) \;=\; α_τ\, ∇_z \log q_φ^{(τ)}(z)\big|_{z = α_τ \tilde{z}} \;=\; α_τ\, s_q^{(τ)}(α_τ \tilde{z}),
\]
hence \(\|s_q^{(τ)}(z)\|^2 = \|\tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}(\tilde{z})\|^2/α_τ^2\) at \(z = α_τ \tilde{z}\).
The expectation transforms by change of variables: for any \(f\),
\(𝔼_{q_φ^{(τ)}}[f(z)] = ∫ q_φ^{(τ)}(z) f(z)\, dz = ∫ \tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\tilde{z})\, f(α_τ \tilde{z})\, d\tilde{z} = 𝔼_{\tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}}[f(α_τ \tilde{z})]\)

c_{\mathrm{out}}(\tilde{σ}_τ) = \frac{\tilde{σ}_τ\, σ_{\mathrm{data}}}{\sqrt{\tilde{σ}_τ^2 + σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2}}, \quad
c_{\mathrm{in}}(\tilde{σ}_τ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\tilde{σ}_τ^2 + σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2}}, \quad
c_{\mathrm{noise}}(\tilde{σ}_τ) = \tfrac{1}{4}\ln \tilde{σ}_τ.\]

**** Regression target and final loss.
Define the regression target
\[F^{*}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)}, τ, c)
:= \frac{D^{*}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)}, τ, c) - c_{\mathrm{skip}}\, \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)}}{c_{\mathrm{out}}}
= \frac{(1 - c_{\mathrm{skip}})\, \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} + \tilde{σ}_τ^2\, \tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} | c)}{c_{\mathrm{out}}}.\]
By construction, \(D^* = c_{\mathrm{skip}}\, \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} + c_{\mathrm{out}}\, F^*\), and the EDM
parameterization writes \(D_ψ = c_{\mathrm{skip}}\, \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} + c_{\mathrm{out}}\, F_ψ\) (same
\(c_{\mathrm{skip}}\) and \(c_{\mathrm{out}}\)). The skip-connection terms cancel in the
difference:
\[
D_ψ - D^{*} \;=\; c_{\mathrm{out}}\, (F_ψ - F^{*}).
\]
Substituting back into the squared score difference,
\[\|\tilde{s}_ψ - \tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}\|^2
= \frac{1}{\tilde{σ}_τ^4}\, \|D_ψ - D^{*}\|^2
= \frac{c_{\mathrm{out}}^2}{\tilde{σ}_τ^4}\, \|F_ψ - F^{*}\|^2,\]
where the first equality uses \(\tilde{s} = (D - \tilde{z})/\tilde{σ}_τ^2\) (from the previous
denoiser-parameterization block).
Combining with the SDE-coefficient factor from the change of variables:
\begin{equation*}
\boxed{\;
\mathcal{L}_t = \tfrac{1}{2}\, 𝔼_{τ \sim π}\, 𝔼_{\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} \sim \tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\cdot | c)}
\!\left[\, w(τ)\,
\bigl\|F_ψ\!\bigl(c_{\mathrm{in}}\, \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)},\, c_{\mathrm{noise}},\, z_{t-1}\bigr)
- F^{*}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)}, τ, c)\bigr\|^2 \,\right],
\;}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
w(τ) \;:=\; \frac{g^2(τ)\, c_{\mathrm{out}}^2(\tilde{σ}_τ)}{α_τ^2\, \tilde{σ}_τ^4}.
\end{equation*}

**** Rescaled target score \(\tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}\).
Recall from the ESM section that the marginal in native coordinates
is \(q_φ^{(τ)}(z_t^{(τ)} | c) = \mathcal{N}(α_τ μ_t,\, α_τ^2 \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + σ_τ^2)\).
Pushed through \(\tilde{z} = z/α_τ\), this becomes
\(\tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} | c) = \mathcal{N}(μ_t,\, \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2)\),
giving the closed-form target
\[\tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} | c)
= -\frac{\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} - μ_t(z_{t-1}, x_{t:T})}{\boldsymbol{σ}_t^2(z_{t-1}, x_{t:T}) + \tilde{σ}_τ^2}.\]
Both \(\tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}\) and \(F^{*}\) are thus available analytically as
functions of the encoder outputs.

**** VP-SDE.
For the VP-SDE with schedule \(β(τ)\):
\[α_τ = \exp(-\tfrac{1}{2}∫_0^τ β(s)\, ds), \quad σ_τ^2 = 1 - α_τ^2, \quad
g^2(τ) = β(τ), \quad \tilde{σ}_τ = \sqrt{α_τ^{-2} - 1}.\]
With normalized latents (\(σ_{\mathrm{data}} = 1\)), the EDM constants reduce to
\[c_{\mathrm{skip}}(τ) = α_τ^2, \quad
c_{\mathrm{out}}(τ) = α_τ\, \tilde{σ}_τ = \sqrt{1 - α_τ^2}, \quad
c_{\mathrm{in}}(τ) = α_τ, \quad
c_{\mathrm{noise}}(τ) = \tfrac{1}{4}\ln \tilde{σ}_τ.\]
The loss weight is
\[w(τ) = \frac{g^2(τ)\, c_{\mathrm{out}}^2(\tilde{σ}_τ)}{α_τ^2\, \tilde{σ}_τ^4}
= \frac{β(τ)\, (1-α_τ^2)}{α_τ^2\, \tilde{σ}_τ^4}
= \frac{β(τ)\, α_τ^2}{1 - α_τ^2}.\]
The marginal in VE coordinates is
\(\tilde{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} | c) = \mathcal{N}(μ_t,\, \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2)\),
sampled by \(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} = μ_t + \sqrt{\boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2} \odot ε\) (element-wise square-root and product),
\(ε \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\), with target score
\[\tilde{s}_q^{(τ)}(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} | c)
= -\frac{\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} - μ_t}{\boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2}.\]

All quantities — \(α_τ\), \(\tilde{σ}_τ\), and the EDM constants — are
deterministic functions of \(β(τ)\) and can be precomputed.

** Baseline Centering
*** Motivation
In numerous settings (cite) it is useful to learn perturbations of a statistical model.
We aim to both simplify the training objective of the diffusion model and provide a smart initialization scheme
by letting our diffusion model learn the residual from some deterministic function of the previous state history, i.e \(δ_t = z_t - m(z_{t_{\mathrm{hist}}})\) for deterministic \(m\). The functional form of \(m\) is itself a design choice: GenCast and CSDI take \(m(z_{t-1}, z_{t-2}) = z_{t-1}\) (identity / random walk); a linear-Gaussian SSM takes \(m(z_{t-1}) = A z_{t-1} + b\); a DKF takes \(m\) to be a learned MLP mean head. We evaluate four parametric families for \(m\) — see [[Baseline-form variants]] below — and present the development with \(μ_p\) standing in for the chosen form (each downstream formula is form-agnostic).

We propose to pretrain the full deep state-space model with a unit-variance
Gaussian transition prior \(p(z_t | z_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(μ_p(z_{t-1}), I)\),
and then /center the diffusion target/:
\[
\hat{z}_t^{(τ)} := \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} - μ_p(z_{t-1}),
\]
where \(μ_p\) is initialized from the stage-1-pretrained baseline (form
selected per [[Baseline-form variants]]) and is either pinned or optionally further
fine-tuned during stage 2 under a prior anchored at the stage-1 value (see
[[Baseline-mode variants]]). The shift is deterministic per \(z_{t-1}\) at each
step, so the centering is a coordinate change at every step: the score in
centered coordinates equals the score of the encoder marginal shifted by the
offset \(μ_p(z_{t-1})\). The score network learns the centered encoder score
directly; the baseline enters the assembled generative model only through
this coordinate shift.


Two design choices follow from this separation:

1. /Mean-only centering./ We center by \(μ_p(z_{t-1})\) but do /not/
   apply a full whitening
   \((\tilde{z}_t - μ_p)/σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\).
   The right scale here is \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\), not the
   per-sample encoder std \(\boldsymbol{σ}_t\): we want the
   coordinate system to be normalized over /all/ data, not per
   batch. But \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\) is defined as a marginal
   over the joint \((p_{\mathrm{data}}, q_φ)\), so it is not
   directly observable — only estimable from samples. Whitening
   the coordinate system by an estimate means the coordinate
   system itself becomes a moving approximation during training,
   and tracking error propagates directly into the diffusion
   objective.

   EDM preconditioning divides by the data variance to keep neural-network inputs unit to variance to. Applying EDM uses \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\) to reparameterize the loss, instead of the coordinate system. As a result, error in
   the tracked \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\) results in sub-optimal preconditioning, still however yielding a valid loss in the fixed coordinate system. /TODO: valid is vauge/
   On the contrary, error in the coordinate system results in the diffusion model being trained on an incorrect objective.

   We note that the EDM preconditioning constants still have an effective divison by \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\), we aim to handle this in the next point.

2. /Unit-variance residual by pretraining; \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}\) as a
   configurable scalar./
   
   To encourage the latent space to initialize with reasonable scaling, we pretrain with the unit-variance prior
   \(\mathcal{N}(μ_p(z_{t-1}), I)\). This pushes the encoder posterior variance
   toward \(I\) and the encoder mean toward \(μ_p\) via the KL loss, so the
   centered residual \(\hat{z}_t^{(0)} = \tilde{z}_t - μ_p(z_{t-1})\) has
   approximately unit per-dimension marginal variance at each \(t\) at the
   close of pretraining: \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t) \approx 1\). Whether this
   continues to hold during stage-2 joint training is an empirical question:
   the encoder's regularization is  due to diffusion rather
   than the pretraining prior, so the residual variance is free to drift.
   We therefore write the EDM constants symbolically in
   \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\) (instead of just assuming a value of \(1\)) and evaluate three configurations for setting it
   (see [[Tracking-mode variants]] below): /(i)/ hold
   \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t) \equiv 1\) (the residual variance is treated as a
   modeling assumption and the loss-conditioning mismatch acts as an implicit
   regularizer); /(ii)/ track a single global EMA scalar; /(iii)/ track a
   per-\(t\) EMA. All three share one estimator and one code path.

   Optionally, we could relax \(\mathcal{N}(μ_p(z_{t-1}), I)\),
   to allow heteroskedasticity via diagonal covariance, instead learning \(\mathcal{N}(μ_p(z_{t-1}), \boldsymbol{σ}_{p}(z_{t-1}))\), where we enforce \(\|\boldsymbol{σ}_p(z_{t-1})\| = 1\) via normalization. Simultaneously, this would allow the variance to change over \(t\) and allow a more flexible inital latent representation via weaker regularization.

*** Baseline-form variants
The centering function \(μ_p(z_{t-1})\) (also written \(m\) in the Motivation)
has not been pinned to a specific functional form. We evaluate four choices,
in increasing capacity. Every following formula in this section applies to
any of the four; substitute the chosen form into \(μ_p\) and proceed.

1. /Zero (\(μ_p \equiv 0\))./ No centering — the diffusion learns the full
   transition density on \(z_t\) directly. The "no residual decomposition"
   baseline of comparison.

2. /Identity (\(μ_p(z_{t-1}) = z_{t-1}\)), as used in GenCast. No parameters; pretraining stage 1 amounts to fitting
   only the encoder and decoder under a random-walk transition prior.

3. /Linear (\(μ_p(z_{t-1}) = A z_{t-1} + b\))./ A pretrained linear-Gaussian
   transition. Fewer parameters (\(A \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}\),
   \(b \in \mathbb{R}^D\)); pretrains quickly. Captures rotations, scalings,
   and translations between consecutive latents.

4. /MLP / DKF mean head./ The pretrained DKF transition mean head, capable
   of representing arbitrary smooth nonlinear means. Highest capacity. This
   is the variant the development is written around; we use \(μ_p\) for it
   generically.

Variants 1 and 2 are parameter-free, so the /Baseline-mode variants/ knob
(pinned vs.\ learnable + prior) is degenerate for them — only "pinned"
applies. Variants 3 and 4 admit both modes.

**** Pairwise comparisons
- /(1) vs.\ (any \(\geq 2\))/: value of residual decomposition at all.
- /(2) vs.\ (3)/: value of learning a baseline beyond identity.
- /(3) vs.\ (4)/: value of nonlinear capacity in the baseline.
- /(1) vs.\ (4)/: full comparison — residual modeling with a pretrained
  nonlinear baseline vs.\ unanchored diffusion.

Pretraining details for each variant differ but all follow the same
template: jointly fit encoder, decoder, and baseline parameters (if any) to
maximize the stage-1 ELBO under the transition prior
\(\mathcal{N}(μ_p(z_{t-1}), I)\). For variants 1 and 2 there are no baseline
parameters to fit; for variants 3 and 4 the baseline is fit alongside the
encoder and decoder.

*** Generative baseline
Let \(μ_p^{(0)}(z_{t-1})\) denote the baseline function at the close of
stage 1 (form chosen per /Baseline-form variants/ above); this seeds the
centered coordinate system
\[
\hat{z}_t^{(τ)} := \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} - μ_p(z_{t-1}),
\]
where \(μ_p\) is initialized at \(μ_p^{(0)}\) and either held fixed or allowed
to continue updating under a prior anchored at \(μ_p^{(0)}\) (see
[[Baseline-mode variants]] below). The baseline does /not/ appear in the
assembled generative score: at sampling time the reverse SDE uses
\(\hat{s}_ψ\) directly. The baseline enters only through this coordinate
change. The transition that snapshots \(μ_p^{(0)}\) and prepares stage 2
is the one-time [[Stage-1 → stage-2 handoff]] subsection below.

**** Baseline-mode variants
We evaluate two configurations.
To motivate these two choices, consider the following argument.
The assembled generative model depends on \(μ_p\) and the diffusion only
through the sampling sum \(z_t = μ_p(z_{t-1}) + \hat{z}_t\). The diffusion model
is strictly more expressive than \(μ_p\), so shifting \(μ_p\) by any function
\(Δ(z_{t-1})\) and simultaneously shifting
\(\mathbb{E}_{\mathrm{diffusion}}[\hat{z}_t | z_{t-1}]\) by \(-Δ(z_{t-1})\)
yields two parameterizations of the same
\(p_ψ(z_t | z_{t-1})\). Without an anchor, the optimizer has a flat direction
along which it can drift while leaving the data likelihood unchanged. Variant
1 fixes the direction, wheras Variant 2's prior anchors it softly, penalizing
free drift.

1. /Pinned (\(μ_p \equiv μ_p^{(0)}\))./ No stage-2 update. The shift is a
   literal, parameter-invariant coordinate change and contributes no gradient
   to anything. No prior is needed because no learning happens. Breaks the
   structural degeneracy below by anchoring \(μ_p\) at the stage-1 point.
   Distinct from the one-time [[stage-1 → stage-2 handoff]] described below,
   which applies whenever we use pretraining.

2. /Learnable with Gaussian anchor./ Allow \(μ_p\) to update during stage 2
   with an added regularizer
   \[
   \mathcal{R}_{μ_p}
   = \tfrac{λ_{μ_p}}{2}\,
     \mathbb{E}_{q_φ(z_{t-1} | x_{1:T})}\!
     \bigl\|μ_p(z_{t-1}) - μ_p^{(0)}(z_{t-1})\bigr\|^2,
   \]
   equivalent to a Gaussian prior
   \(μ_p(\cdot) \sim \mathcal{N}\bigl(μ_p^{(0)}(\cdot),\, λ_{μ_p}^{-1} I\bigr)\)
   on the function output evaluated under the encoder. Evaluated on trajectory
   samples \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\) already drawn for the ELBO; gradient on
   \(z_{t-1}^{(s)}\) is stopped so \(\mathcal{R}_{μ_p}\) contributes only to
   \(μ_p\) (and not back through the encoder).

**** Interaction with tracking-mode variants
Allowing learnable \(μ_p\) will possibly reduce the spread of residual
means, i.e the term \(\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{Var}[\hat{μ}_t])\) used to compute \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) will be reduced since
\(μ_p\) is now free to track \(\mathbb{E}[μ_t]\).
The choices of how to track \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) and whether or not to allow learnable \(μ_{p}\) therefore interact,
so we run the full \(2 \times 3\) grid as an ablation study.
A possible prediction: under
learnable \(μ_p\), the gap between tracking-mode variants (2) and (3) should
shrink, because the residual-mean drift that variant (3) is designed to
absorb has already been absorbed by \(μ_p\).

*** Stage-1 → stage-2 handoff 
We now describe the transition from stage-1 baseline training to stage-2 diffusion training.
The handoff fixes the baseline snapshot, resets optimizer state, perturbs
the encoder, initializes the data-variance buffers, and swaps the loss.

The steps below execute in order at the boundary between the two stages.

1. /Stage-1 termination./ Stop after \(N_{\mathrm{pretrain}}\) steps (tuned)
   or when the stage-1 ELBO improvement over a sliding window drops below a
   threshold. Log the residual diagnostics already required by
   /Practical considerations/ — \(\mathbb{E}[\|\boldsymbol{σ}_t\|^2]\), \(\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{Var}[\hat{μ}_t])\), and
   \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) — to verify the near-unit-variance close
   that the centering scheme assumes.

2. /Snapshot \(μ_p^{(0)}\)./ Deep-copy baseline parameters. 
   
3. /Rebuild optimizers./ Discard Adam first- and
   second-moment buffers and the step counter; instantiate fresh
   optimizer(s) over the stage-2 parameter groups. The rationale here is that the
   Stage-1 moments are calibrated to the reconstruction + Gaussian-KL loss surface.
   In stage-2, the diffusion gradients yield substantially different structure in the encoder gradients
   as the regularization pressure has become substantially weakened. 

4. /Inject encoder parameter noise — full encoder weights./ Perturb all
   encoder parameters:
   \[
   φ \;\leftarrow\; φ + σ_{\mathrm{pert}} \cdot ε,
   \qquad ε \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I).
   \]
   The intention here is to allow the encoder to escape a sharp local minimum
   due to the srict regularization in stage-1. 
   A small perturbation lets stage 2 reorganize the encoder without first
   having to escape stage-1 geometry. The magnitude is tuned
   \(σ_{\mathrm{pert}}\) alongside other hyperparameters.

5. /Initialize \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t]\) by inheriting from stage 1./
   In all cells we initialize \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t] \leftarrow σ_p^2[t]\):
   stage 1 produces \(σ_p^2[t]\) (which equals \(1\) for the
   identity/homoskedastic prior, recovering the \(σ_{\mathrm{data}} \equiv 1\)
   start), so the two descriptions are equivalent. If the cell uses
   /Fixed/ tracking the buffer remains at this initial value;
   /Learnable with Gaussian anchor/ regularizer.
   otherwise the EMA proceeds from it.

6. /Optional stage-2 LR warmup?/ Brief linear warmup over the first
   \(N_{\mathrm{warmup}}\) steps. Motivated by step 4: large updates
   immediately after a fresh perturbation can compound and destabilize.
   We currently are not using LR scheduling, however it is common in many works. 

7. /Swap loss./ Replace the stage-1 transition KL \(KL[q_φ \,\|\,
   \mathcal{N}(μ_p, I)]\) with the ESM/EDM transition-KL surrogate of
   /Regression target and final loss/ below.

*** Centered coordinates and encoder marginal
Building on the EDM rescaling \(\tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} = z_t^{(τ)} / α_τ\) and
\(\tilde{σ}_τ = σ_τ / α_τ\) of the previous section, define the centered
coordinate
\[
\hat{z}_t^{(τ)} := \tilde{z}_t^{(τ)} - μ_p(z_{t-1}).
\]
The shift is deterministic and state-dependent, so the effective noise
level remains \(\tilde{σ}_τ\). The encoder marginal in centered coordinates is
\[
\hat{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\hat{z}_t^{(τ)} | c)
= \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\hat{μ}_t,\; \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2\bigr),
\]
where \(μ_t, \boldsymbol{σ}_t\) are the encoder posterior mean and std, and
\[
\hat{μ}_t := μ_t(c) - μ_p(z_{t-1})
\]
is the observation-conditional residual mean. The pretraining prior
\(\mathcal{N}(μ_p, I)\) drives \(\hat{μ}_t \approx 0\) and
\(\boldsymbol{σ}_t \approx \mathbf{1}\) at the close of stage 1, so at
\(τ = 0\) the diffusion model sees approximately zero-mean unit-variance
inputs. \(\hat{μ}_t\) is the structure \(F_ψ\) is trained to model above
and beyond the prior mean.

*** Data-variance tracking
EDM preconditioning needs the variance of the clean
data distribution, \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2\). In our setting the "clean data" at diffusion
time \(τ = 0\) is the centered residual \(\hat{z}_t^{(0)}\), whose marginal
distribution under \((p_{\mathrm{data}}, q_φ)\) /a priori/ varies with the
latent index \(t\). The most general formulation treats
\(σ_{\mathrm{data}}\) as a function of \(t\),
\[
σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)
:= \frac{1}{D}\, \mathrm{tr}\!\left(\mathrm{Var}_{p_{\mathrm{data}},\, q_φ}\!\bigl[\hat{z}_t^{(0)}\bigr]\right),
\qquad t = 2, \ldots, T,
\]
where \(D\) is the latent dimension. By the law of total variance and the fact
that the encoder posterior is Gaussian with diagonal covariance,
\[
σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)
= \frac{1}{D}\Bigl(\,\underbrace{𝔼\!\bigl[\|\boldsymbol{σ}_t\|^2\bigr]}_{\text{average posterior variance}}
\;+\;\underbrace{\mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(\mathrm{Var}\!\bigl[\hat{μ}_t\bigr]\bigr)}_{\text{spread of residual means}}\Bigr).
\]
The decomposition surfaces one of the goals of pretraining: stage-1 regularization drives
\(\boldsymbol{σ}_t \to \mathbf{1}\) (so the first term \(\to 1\)) and
\(\hat{μ}_t \to 0\) (so the second term \(\to 0\)), hence
\(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t) \to 1\) at the close of stage 1. During stage 2 either
term can shift. [[Tracking-mode variants]] below picks between three ways to
populate \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\) during stage 2.

**** Estimator
A minibatch yields \(N\) trajectory samples and hence \(N\) draws of
\((\hat{μ}_{t,i}, \boldsymbol{σ}_{t,i})\) per \(t\) from the encoder analytic
moments. The per-batch estimator
\(\bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) targets object (3) from the
[[Notation: three variance objects]] list — the data-marginal variance
\(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t) = \tfrac{1}{D}\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{Var}_{p_{\mathrm{data}}, q_φ}[\hat{z}_t^{(0)}]).\)

**** EMA update and gradient flow
We keep a buffer \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t]\) for each \(t \in \{2, \ldots, T\}\),
updated as
\[
σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t] \;\leftarrow\; γ\, σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t] \;+\; (1 - γ)\, \bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t),
\qquad γ \in [0.99,\, 0.9999],
\]
initialized at the start of stage 2 from \(σ_p^2[t]\) per the /Stage-1 → stage-2 handoff/ protocol (step 5).
The EMA is detached from the autograd graph: the value used to form the EDM
constants is \(\mathrm{stop\_grad}(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t])\), so no gradient
flows to \(φ\) depending on \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t]\).

**** Tracking-mode variants
We evaluate three configurations, nested in expressivity. They share the
estimator \(\bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) above and one code path, differing
only in (a) whether the EMA update is applied and (b) whether updates are
pooled across \(t\).

1. /Fixed (\(σ_{\mathrm{data}} \equiv 1\))./ Hold
   \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t] \equiv 1\) and skip the EMA update. The
   loss-conditioning is calibrated to the /assumed/ residual variance, not
   the /actual/ one — so any drift of the encoder away from
   \(\boldsymbol{σ}_t \approx \mathbf{1}\) is implicitly penalized via
   mis-scaled gradients. This relies on stage-1 pretraining to put the encoder
   near unit-variance residuals and requires that the encoder does not drift in order to retain
   optimal preconditioning.

2. /Global EMA./ Tie all per-\(t\) buffers to a single scalar
   \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2\), updated by
   \(\bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2 = \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\)
   over the timesteps \(\mathcal{T}\) visited at each step. Preconditioning
   adapts to encoder drift but assumes residual variance is approximately
   \(t\)-homogeneous. Removes the implicit regularizer of (1) without paying
   the \(t\)-resolution cost of (3).

3. /Per-\(t\) EMA./ Independent buffers \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t]\),
   \(t = 2, \ldots, T\), updated as in /EMA update and gradient flow/ above.
   Lets the preconditioning track any \(t\)-dependent structure in the
   residual variance — including structure that emerges during stage 2 as
   the encoder adapts to the diffusion-induced transition.

To make the comparisons direct we log the raw per-batch
\(\bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) under all three runs, even when its value
isn't used in the loss:
- (1) vs.\ (2) isolates the value of letting the preconditioning track
  encoder drift (and the cost of losing the implicit unit-variance
  regularizer).
- (2) vs.\ (3) isolates the value of \(t\)-resolution in the tracked scale.
- (1) vs.\ (3) is the headline comparison: free encoder + flexible
  preconditioning vs.\ pretraining-anchored encoder.

*** Encoder score target
A pure shift coordinate shift leaves the score invariant — \(\hat{s}(\hat{z}) = \tilde{s}(\hat{z} + μ_p)\)
— so the closed-form encoder score in centered coordinates is
\[
\hat{s}_q^{(τ)}(\hat{z}_t^{(τ)} | c)
= -\frac{\hat{z}_t^{(τ)} - \hat{μ}_t(c)}{Σ_t^{(τ)}},
\qquad Σ_t^{(τ)} := \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2.
\]
The generative score is parameterized by the network directly:
\(\hat{s}_ψ(\hat{z}_t^{(τ)}, τ, z_{t-1})\). We apply the EDM denoiser
parameterization with the tracked, per-\(t\) data scale
\(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)\):
\[
c_{\mathrm{skip}}(\tilde{σ}_τ;\, t) = \frac{σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)}{\tilde{σ}_τ^2 + σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)}, \qquad
c_{\mathrm{out}}(\tilde{σ}_τ;\, t) = \frac{\tilde{σ}_τ\, σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t)}{\sqrt{\tilde{σ}_τ^2 + σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)}},
\]
\[
c_{\mathrm{in}}(\tilde{σ}_τ;\, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\tilde{σ}_τ^2 + σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)}}, \qquad
c_{\mathrm{noise}}(\tilde{σ}_τ) = \tfrac{1}{4}\ln \tilde{σ}_τ.
\]
At \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t) = 1\) these recover the canonical EDM constants from
the previous section. The three variance objects from /Notation: three
variance objects/ each have a separate role here:
\(\boldsymbol{σ}_t^2\) (object 1) enters the analytic score
\(\hat{s}_q^{(τ)}\) via the per-sample variance
\(Σ_t^{(τ)} = \boldsymbol{σ}_t^2 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2\)
(object 2); \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) (object 3) enters only the EDM
preconditioning constants.

*** Regression target and final loss
Substituting into the squared score difference and accounting for the
change of variables, the per-step loss becomes
\begin{equation*}
\boxed{\;
\mathcal{L}_t = \tfrac{1}{2}\, 𝔼_{τ \sim π}\,
𝔼_{\hat{z}_t^{(τ)} \sim \hat{q}_φ^{(τ)}(\cdot | c)}
\!\left[\, w(τ;\, t)\,
\bigl\|F_ψ\!\bigl(c_{\mathrm{in}}(\tilde{σ}_τ;\, t)\, \hat{z}_t^{(τ)},\,
c_{\mathrm{noise}},\, z_{t-1}\bigr)
- F^{*}(\hat{z}_t^{(τ)}, τ, c)\bigr\|^2 \,\right],
\;}
\end{equation*}
with regression target
\[
F^{*}(\hat{z}_t^{(τ)}, τ, c)
= \frac{(1 - c_{\mathrm{skip}})\, \hat{z}_t^{(τ)} +
\tilde{σ}_τ^2\, \hat{s}_q^{(τ)}}{c_{\mathrm{out}}},
\]
and loss weight
\[
w(τ;\, t)
= \frac{g^2(τ)\, c_{\mathrm{out}}^2(\tilde{σ}_τ;\, t)}
{α_τ^2\, \tilde{σ}_τ^4}
= \frac{g^2(τ)\, σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)}
{α_τ^2\, \tilde{σ}_τ^2\, \bigl(\tilde{σ}_τ^2 + σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\bigr)}.
\]
At \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}(t) = 1\) the weight reduces to
\(g^2(τ)/(α_τ^2 \tilde{σ}_τ^2 (1 + \tilde{σ}_τ^2))\), the EDM
expression from above. /TODO: ref eqn/
Both \(\hat{s}_q^{(τ)}\) (from the centered encoder marginal, using
\(\boldsymbol{σ}_t^2\)) and the EDM constants (using \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\))
are analytic in \((\hat{z}_t^{(τ)}, c, z_{t-1}, τ)\) given the tracked EMA, so
\(F^{*}\) is available in closed form. The state dependence enters only
through \(μ_p\) (in the centering) and through \(\boldsymbol{σ}_t\) in the
analytic score; the weight \(w(τ; t)\) is \(z_{t-1}\)-independent. The encoder
parameters \(φ\) receive gradient through the sampling distribution
\(\hat{q}_φ^{(τ)}\) (via reparameterization), through the analytic target
\(\hat{s}_q^{(τ)}\), and through the reconstruction loss. The tracked
\(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) is held constant w.r.t. \(φ\) — no gradient flows
back through the EMA.


*** Practical considerations
The diagnostic bullet below applies to all three tracking-mode variants; the
remaining bullets apply only when an EMA is in use (Variants 2 and 3).
- *Drift diagnostic (all variants).* Per \(t\), log the empirical mean of
  \(\hat{μ}_t\) and the raw per-batch \(\bar{σ}_{\mathrm{data}}^2(t)\) (along
  with its two components from the variance decomposition). Under Variants 2
  and 3 also log the EMA buffer \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t]\). Mean drift
  indicates how far the encoder posterior mean has moved from the pretrained
  prior mean; variance drift is absorbed by the EMA in Variants 2 and 3 so
  preconditioning self-corrects, but is /not/ absorbed under Variant 1, where
  large drift signals a real conditioning problem. 
- *Initialization and warmup.* Initialize \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t] = σ_p^2[t]\) (initialize from the baseline transition variance) at the
  start of stage 2.
- *Sampling.* At sampling time, the same EMA buffer is used for the EDM
  constants (with \(σ_{\mathrm{data}}^2[t]\) frozen at its last-train value).
  If sampling at \(t\) outside the training horizon, fall back to the nearest
  trained \(t\) (or to \(σ_{\mathrm{data}} = 1\)).

* Misc quotes from the LSGM paper to remember
** On Sampling Speed Improvements
We identify three main reasons for this significantly faster sampling from LSGM: (i) The SGM prior
in our LSGM models latent variables with 32×32 spatial dim., whereas the original SGM [2] directly
models 256×256 images. The larger spatial dimensions require a deeper network to achieve a large
receptive field. (ii) Inspecting the SGM prior in our model suggests that the score function is heavily
dominated by the linear term at the end of training, as the mixing coefficients α are all < 0.02. This
makes our SGM prior smooth and numerically faster to solve. (iii) Since SGM is formed in the latent
space in our model, errors from solving the ODE can be corrected to some degree using the VAE
decoder, while in the original SGM [2] errors directly translate to artifacts in pixel space.
** Usefulness to Us
The results mentioned above show 637x and 56x speedups while achieving better FID results. In our case, we expect to
realize this advantage as well over obs. space diffusion time-series, because 1. we operate in latent space, but also 2. we reduce the temporal
dimension. 
Note that we may or may not want to use the "mixture" of scores used in their model. 

** End-to-End training
End-to-end training. We proposed to train LSGM end-to-end, in contrast to [ 10]. Using a similar
setup as above we compare end-to-end training of LSGM during the second stage with freezing the
VAE encoder and decoder and only training the SGM prior in latent space during the second stage.
When training the model end-to-end, we achieve an FID of 5.19 and NELBO of 2.98; when freezing
the VAE networks during the second stage, we only get an FID of 9.00 and NELBO of 3.03. These
results clearly motivate our end-to-end training strategy.
